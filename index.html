Beneath the serene surface of the Great Lakes, one of the world's largest freshwater systems, conceals an unsettling truth—a looming threat of an oil spill from a 70-year-old pipeline. Built to last no longer than 50 years, the pipeline still pushes millions of gallons of oil each hour, despite the warnings by the scientists and environmental activists.

Enbridge Energy's Line 5 pipeline stretches over 4.5 miles under the Straits of Mackinac, where two of the Great Lakes—Lake Michigan and Lake Huron—meet. It is also a culturally important and sacred place to the Anishinaabe—a group of tribes that first inhabited the Great Lakes region.

Despite multiple efforts by the state of Michigan and other activist groups to shut down the pipeline, the Canadian company refuses to put their operations to a halt.

At the forefront of this fight to shut down Line 5 stand the native Great Lakes tribes.
